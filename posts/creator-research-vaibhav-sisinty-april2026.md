## Hook Formulas (6 patterns — from YouTube transcripts, highest-performing)

All 6 come from notebooklm analysis of his most-viewed YouTube videos. These exact patterns drive 200K–800K views.

---

**Pattern 1: High-Stakes Financial Impact Hook**
The number is always massive and tied to a single event.

> "OpenAI just declared a code red — Nvidia lost $600 billion in a single day. The biggest single-day loss in stock market history."
> "One blog post from Anthropic wiped $31 billion off IBM's stock in a single day."
> "Anthropic just dropped a model so good it crashed the stock market."

**For Sumanth:** "Snitch spent ₹2 Cr on paid ads in Q4. Their organic reach is still zero. Here's why one campaign can't fix a broken distribution model."

---

**Pattern 2: Secret/Unknown Tool Hook**
Preys on FOMO — powerful things exist that your competition already uses, but you don't know about yet.

> "There is one feature in AI right now that will save you more time than anything else. It's not prompting. It's not agents. And 99% of people have never even heard of it."
> "Google has two free tools that most people do not even know exist."
> "Stop paying for AI. I mean it. Google just quietly released their entire professional-grade AI arsenal — and the whole thing is absolutely free."

**For Sumanth:** "There's one distribution move Indian D2C brands are still not making. It's not influencers. It's not paid ads. And the brands that find it first will compound for years."

---

**Pattern 3: Shocking AI Feat Hook**
Sci-fi-level action that AI performed autonomously. Proves AI is no longer just a chatbot.

> "This week, 1 million AI agents built their own society. They created a religion, a secret language. One got itself a phone number and started calling its human."
> "Something satisfying is happening on the internet right now — people are hiring AI employees that actually do the work."

**For Sumanth:** "A Bengaluru D2C brand got 2 million views last week without a single ad rupee. Here's exactly how the content compounded."

---

**Pattern 4: Tech Giant Drama Hook**
Frames the story as a war between titans. Makes it sound like breaking news.

> "Disney just handed its most iconic character — Mickey Mouse — to AI. I'm not even kidding. They just signed a billion-dollar deal with OpenAI."
> "OpenAI just fixed its biggest mistake. China dropped two open-source models that beat GPT-5. And the battle that decides how you'll shop online is happening right now in a courtroom."

**For Sumanth:** "Mamaearth spent ₹90 Cr on influencer marketing last year. Meta got most of it. The creators got crumbs. Here's who actually wins."

---

**Pattern 5: Relatable Pain Point & Cost Saving Hook**
Calls out a specific, frustrating problem the viewer is currently paying for — then introduces a free or better alternative.

> "You're still paying for 10 different AI tools. Stop. Just stop. Google just dropped the biggest updates to NotebookLM ever."
> "Cursor charges ₹1,700/month. Lovable wants ₹2,200. Emergent wants your money before you can build anything. Google just dropped a vibe coding tool that's completely free."

**For Sumanth:** "Indian brands are paying ₹3–5 lakh per influencer campaign for content that dies in 72 hours. There's now a model where you pay nothing upfront and only pay per verified view."

---

**Pattern 6: Urgency / "While You Were Sleeping" Hook**
Emphasizes extreme speed. Makes the viewer feel like the world changed overnight.

> "While you were sleeping, OpenAI just launched a new app store inside ChatGPT. Guess what — it has 800 million active users on day one."
> "Sam Altman just leaked the most important date in human history and nobody's paying attention."
> "Every generation gets one moment where the rules change without warning. In 1972, calculators hit offices. Right now something is happening to knowledge workers and most people haven't noticed yet."

**For Sumanth:** "While D2C founders debated CPM vs CPC last quarter, 3 brands quietly built audiences they own. Here's what they did differently."

## CTA Bank (7 patterns — from LinkedIn posts with 50+ comments)

Data source: Direct analysis of Vaibhav's 25 highest-comment LinkedIn posts (40–117 comments each).

---

**CTA Type 1: Participation CTA (highest comment driver)**
Ask if you should release something. Turns a content decision into content itself.

> "Also, managed to shoot the conversation. Should I drop the recording here?" — Post 48, 97 comments
> "What do you want us to cover in Episode 3? Let me know in comments." — Post 12, 60 comments
> "A few days ago I asked if I should drop the recording. The response was loud. So here it is." — Post 35, 59 comments

**Rule:** Ask yes/no or preference questions about YOUR next move. The audience feels ownership over your content.

---

**CTA Type 2: P.S. Bombshell**
Drop a second, even more provocative data point at the very end as a P.S. Reignites reading. Drives comments on the PS itself.

> "P.S. Anthropic is opening a Bengaluru office this year. They're not just disrupting Indian IT. They're hiring from it." — Post 39
> "P.S. The platform that crashed on day one because too many AI agents signed up is now owned by the company with 3.3 billion human users. Think about what happens when those two networks merge." — Post 38
> "P.S. Over 100,000 people have now listed themselves on the platform. They're not waiting for AI to take their jobs — they're applying to work for it." — Post 45

**Rule:** The P.S. should make a point that contradicts or complicates the main post. It's a bonus hook, not a summary.

---

**CTA Type 3: Yes/No Opinion Question**
Simple enough to answer in 5 words. Forces a stance.

> "Do you think this is the beginning of the post-smartphone era or just another hardware experiment?" — Post 42, 79 comments

**Rule:** The question must have two defensible sides. Avoid questions where everyone agrees.

---

**CTA Type 4: Hard Landing (no CTA)**
Some of his highest-comment posts have zero CTA. The post just ends on the most provocative line.

> "It's whether the tasks that justify your seat still need a human sitting in it." — Post 17, 53 comments
> "One of those groups is getting smaller every quarter." — Post 37, 50 comments
> "The question isn't whether AI will change the world. It already is." — Post 22, 117 comments

**Rule:** Hard landings work when the final line is so uncomfortable that people are compelled to respond. No question needed — the implicit question is "Am I in the group that's shrinking?"

---

**CTA Type 5: "What would you do?" / Preference CTA**
Asks the reader to project themselves into a choice.

> "P.S. What skill would you build first?" — Post 19, 51 comments

**For Sumanth:** "If your brand could only own one distribution channel for the next 3 years — what would it be?"

---

**CTA Type 6: Community Funnel CTA**
Soft conversion to WhatsApp community. Appears in posts with 40–73 comments, usually combined with another CTA type.

> "P.S. I share updates like this in my free AI community on WhatsApp. Link in the first comment ↓"
> "I post breakdowns like this daily in my community. Join here: [link]"

**Rule for Sumanth:** Don't use this yet — no community exists. Replace with "DM me if your brand is doing this and it's working (or failing)."

---

**CTA Type 7: Reverse Challenge / Proof Invitation**
Invites people to prove you wrong or prove you right with their own example.

> (implied in posts like Post 14): "Focus is not a strategy. Focus IS the strategy. Thoughts?"

**For Sumanth:** "Has your brand run a campaign that actually compounded past the first week? What made it different? Drop it below — I'll break it down."

## Post Structure Pattern (LinkedIn)

This is the exact 6-beat structure Vaibhav uses in his highest-performing LinkedIn posts.

```
BEAT 1 — SHOCK HOOK (1–2 lines)
Number + dramatic fact. Often combined with emoji.
Max 120 chars. Must create dissonance.
"Recruiters charge ₹30,000–₹50,000 to do something Claude just figured out in 3 minutes. 🤯"

BEAT 2 — PATTERN INTERRUPT (2–4 lines)
"Not X. Not Y. Not Z." — dismisses what the reader expects this to be about.
Sets up the specific angle.
"And most job seekers still don't know this exists."

BEAT 3 — EVIDENCE DELIVERY (arrow bullets)
→ Each bullet = one revelation
→ Specific, not general ("Step 1: Connect Indeed to Claude")
→ Present tense. Active. No passive constructions.
→ Short lines. Each bullet stands alone.
Typically 4–8 bullets.

BEAT 4 — "HERE'S WHAT NOBODY'S TELLING YOU" PIVOT
The contrarian turn. Usually 1–2 sentences.
"Here is the thing." / "Now here's the part that should worry every Indian professional:"
This is where the POV lives. Everything before is evidence. This is the verdict.

BEAT 5 — THE REFRAME (2–4 lines)
The insight stated plainly and memorably.
"We're not moving from human work to AI work. We're moving from knowledge premium to presence premium."
"The jobs that can't be done with an internet connection are the ones that will command premium pricing."
One sentence per idea. No explanations. Trust the reader.

BEAT 6 — CLOSE (pick one)
Option A: P.S. Bombshell — drops a contradictory data point
Option B: Participation CTA — "Should I drop the recording?"
Option C: Opinion question — "Do you think...?"
Option D: Hard landing — most provocative statement, no CTA
Option E: Community link — only after community is established
```

**Average length:** 800–1,500 characters. Roughly 150–280 words.
**Format rule:** Every paragraph = 1–2 sentences max. Double line break between every paragraph.
**Never:** 1–3 word orphan lines (Twitter-poetry). Long unbroken paragraphs. Passive voice.

## What NOT to Do (Vaibhav's failed patterns)

1. **Minimal text + image only** — Post 3 (293 likes, 36 comments): Just "something for all of us to introspect on!" + tweet screenshot. Does okay but far below his potential. Always add analysis.

2. **No pivot/reframe** — Posts that just describe what happened without the "here's what nobody's telling you" turn perform 30–40% below average. The contrarian framing IS the engagement driver.

3. **Selling directly on personal channels** — He explicitly said he almost never mentions Growth School on his personal content. The moment people sense a sales agenda, the trust collapses. He routes sales through paid ads, not organic content.

4. **Telling people to share/like** — Zero "like this post if you agree" CTAs in any of his 50 posts. He never begs for engagement. His CTAs either invite participation or drop a bombshell.

5. **Generic AI commentary without specificity** — His lowest-performing posts describe broad AI trends. His highest-performing posts name the company, the date, the exact dollar amount, the specific person who said it. Specificity = credibility = engagement.


## Top 3 Insights to Apply This Week

**Insight 1 — The "Should I drop it?" CTA is under-used and extremely effective**
Post 48 ended with "Should I drop the recording here?" — 97 comments, his highest-comment non-existential post. Post 35 ("A few days ago I asked if I should drop the recording. The response was loud.") got 878 likes off the follow-up. 

This works because it transfers content ownership to the audience. They feel they caused the content to exist.

**For Sumanth this week:** End your next post with a participation question about YOUR next move. Not "what do you think about X" — ask about what you should do next. "I've been tracking 12 D2C brands this month. Should I share what I found?"

---

**Insight 2 — P.S. Bombshells are his secret weapon**
Every high-comment post ends with either a hard landing statement or a P.S. that drops a contradictory data point. The P.S. is designed to be more surprising than the post itself.

Look at Post 39: The whole post is about Anthropic crashing IT stocks. The P.S.? "Anthropic is opening a Bengaluru office this year. They're not just disrupting Indian IT. They're hiring from it." That P.S. drives more comments than the body because it forces a response.

**For Sumanth this week:** After writing your next post, ask: "What's the one thing that makes this more complicated or contradictory that I left out?" Put that in a P.S.

---

**Insight 3 — Macro Fear + Specific Indian Context = His Highest-Engagement Formula**
His top posts in the first 30 days combined:
- A macro fear hook with a massive number (₹2 lakh crore, IBM $31B, 108,000 jobs)
- With a specifically Indian interpretation ("This is what it means for Indian IT professionals")
- With a career survival message ("Here's what you need to do before this reaches you")

This is the "global story + India-specific consequence + individual action" structure. It's the same structure Sumanth should use for distribution: "Meta launched [new format]. Indian D2C brands are still running TV-ad scripts on it. Here's why that's costing them ₹X per day."

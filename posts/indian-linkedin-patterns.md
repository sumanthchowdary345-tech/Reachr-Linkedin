# What Makes Indian LinkedIn Audiences Comment (Not Just Like)

**Source:** Perplexity analysis of 9 real high-engagement Indian founder posts (April 2026)
**Purpose:** Use these structural triggers when writing every LinkedIn post

---

## The Core Finding

A liked post makes people feel something.
A commented post makes people want to say something publicly — usually because it touches their identity, professional reputation, or tribal belonging.

The structural difference isn't topic. It's whether the post gives people a reason to **speak on record**.

---

## 7 Proven Comment Triggers (Verified from Real Indian Posts)

### Trigger 1 — Identity Stake
**What it does:** Forces people to classify themselves as "in the right camp" or defend their position.

**Template:**
> "There are two types of [role] in India right now — [Type A] and [Type B]. Which are you?"

**Verified example:** Ankur Warikoo's "2025 is going to reward a very specific kind of professional" — 181 comments. The hook created in-group/out-group. People commented to signal they're in the right group.

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "There are two types of Indian D2C marketing heads in 2026 — those still paying influencers upfront, and those who only pay when views actually happen. Which camp is your brand in?"

---

### Trigger 2 — Unresolved Tension (Two Sides Left Open)
**What it does:** Post presents two legitimate positions and doesn't resolve them. Readers rush in to resolve it.

**Template:**
> "[Thing A] is happening. But [Thing B] is also true. Which one wins?"

**Verified example:** Warikoo's "India addicted to mindless scrolling" — 135 comments. Set up pessimist India vs. optimist India and didn't pick a side. Both camps arrived.

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "Indian brands are starting to realize that influencer marketing isn't broken. The pricing model is. Agree or disagree?"

---

### Trigger 3 — Specific Rupee Amount in Hook
**What it does:** Specificity demands explanation. Vague claims don't. Specific numbers invite verification, debate, and calculation.

**Template:**
> "₹[X] on [action]. Here's what happened."

**Verified examples:**
- Shashank Mehta: "Took 250 folks to Goa. Spent ₹45 lakhs. ROI = Infinite." — very high comments
- Jatin Saini's list posts — 198 comments

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "A D2C brand paid ₹0 upfront and got 200 creator videos in 12 days. Here's the math."

---

### Trigger 4 — Vulnerability BEFORE the Lesson
**What it does:** When someone with status drops the posture, it opens space for others to share real stories. Comments become personal stories, not just reactions.

**Template:**
> "I [failure/unexpected outcome]. Here's what I actually learned: [non-obvious lesson]."

**Verified example:** Shashank Mehta's Goa trip: "I almost cancelled it" buried inside a post about spending ₹45L. The vulnerability earned moral authority for the contrarian claim.

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "Week 4: A brand rejected our content rewards pilot because 'we don't trust creators we don't pick ourselves.' Here's why that stung — and what we're changing in the platform because of it."

---

### Trigger 5 — Name Real Brands / People in a List
**What it does:** Every name-tag cross-pollinates audiences. Named people + their teams come to comment. People add names you missed. Each tag expands reach to audiences you don't own yet.

**Verified example:** Jatin Saini "Top 50 startup founders" — 198 comments. Gratitude + omission loop.

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "30 Indian D2C brands actually doing UGC right now (and not just throwing money at celebrities). Inside the carousel: [Brand A], [Brand B], [Brand C]..."

---

### Trigger 6 — Open CTA That Implies the Reader's Opinion Matters
**What it does:** Moves passivity to participation. The key phrase is "Anything I missed?" or "Which one is yours?" — implies the commenter has expertise to add, not just an opinion.

**Template:**
> "[Claim or list]. Anything I missed, or something you'd swap out? Tell me in the comments 👇"

**Verified example:** Aroona Chawla's "The Real D2C Stack for Indian Founders" — 43 comments. CTA was "Anything I missed, should try, or switch to?" Comments = contributors, not consumers.

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "Which product category do you think is most underserved by current influencer marketing in India? Drop one brand below 👇"

---

### Trigger 7 — Fault Line Framing (Two Tribes in Conflict)
**What it does:** Names a conflict both sides feel strongly about. Both tribes arrive to defend their position. Not "here's a problem" — "here's a fight."

**Template:**
> "The real battle in [space] isn't [obvious thing]. It's [hidden conflict] vs [hidden conflict]."

**Verified example:** Vineeta Singh "50M creators, fragile brand-creator equation" — 50+ comments. Named the brands-vs-creators tension. Both groups commented to defend.

**Reachr adaptation:**
> "The real fight in Indian FMCG marketing isn't TV vs digital. It's finance teams vs marketing teams — one wants predictable spends, the other wants creator content. Content Rewards is literally a peace treaty between them."

## The Comment Trigger Scorecard (Before Every Post)

Before publishing, check your post against this:

| Trigger | Present? |
|---|---|
| Identity stake ("which type are you?") | Y/N |
| Unresolved tension (two valid sides) | Y/N |
| Specific ₹ number in hook | Y/N |
| Vulnerability before the lesson | Y/N |
| Real brand/person names with tags | Y/N |
| "Anything I missed?" style CTA | Y/N |
| Fault line framing (two tribes) | Y/N |

**Target:** At least 2 of these 7 per post. A post with 0 will get likes and no comments.

## What NOT to Do (Anti-Patterns That Kill Comments)

- "What do you think?" — too vague. Nobody knows how to answer publicly.
- "Share your thoughts below" — passive, no stakes
- Generic educational content with no identity threat
- Perfectly balanced "on one hand, on the other hand" posts — no tribe to join
- Posts that only agree with the reader — agreement doesn't generate debate
- Posts that make a claim but provide no evidence or story — no hook to hang a comment on
